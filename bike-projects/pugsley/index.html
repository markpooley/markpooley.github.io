My Surly Pugsley was in desparate need of attention. Sometime in 2012 I procurred this bike from a friend (who was looking to subsidize an upgrade). After one winter of crusty drivetrain issues I elected to convert it to a single speed. Last winter it was ridden hard and put away wet - literally.

When I attempted to move it this summer I discovered that nearly everthing was seized up on it. Breaks, chain, headset were all bound. I kicked myself for neglecting this trusty bike and letting it fall into this state.

I musted the necessary gumption to give it a thorough overhaul. While systematically dismantling everthing on it I took a good long look at the condition of the frame. After quick call to RFE powder coating to get a verbal estimate I decided this bike deserved a total overhaul. Here's the documentation that process.

Stripping Down the Frame and Assessing the Components

This is one of the first generation Puglseys with an offset front fork - making the front and rear wheels swapable. I'm sure my buddy rode it just a much and just as hard before it came into my posession. Originally I had 9 speed drivetrain in the rear, with a double chain ring up front, and a Surly Ultra New 135 mm hub on the front wheel. However the track dropouts on the rear make taking a wheel off a pain if you have a derailleur - which only provided further impetus for converting it to single speed (SS).

Here's some photos of the bike before and while tearing it down. Click on any of the thumbnails for a larger version.

Sending the Frame to Powder Coat

Chris at RFE powdercoating did one hell of a job blasting and treating this sorry old frame. He discovered "SNOW" was part of the number system on the Bottom Bracket, which is pretty damn cool.

Chris has been great to work with. He's reasonable in price, has a quick turn around time, and sent me a library of photos he took from working on the frame

Building it Back Up

I built it back up a little different. I built a new rear wheel using a Marge Lite Rim, Wheelsmith double butted spokes<, and a Surly Ultra New Fixed Disc Hub. That's right, a fixed fat bike! I wanted to strip this down to the simplest possible machine I could. Building a new wheel also freed up a Holy Rolling Darryl for my wife's Pugsley. I used Free Spoke to figure out spoke lengths, and had a friend cut them.
